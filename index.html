Xiaohan Wu

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Political Science at the University of California, San Diego, specializing in Computational Social Science. I am advised by Molly Roberts and affiliated with the Halıcıoğlu Data Science Institute.

My research sits at the intersection of political communication, law, and computational social science. My dissertation explores how digital information flows—from government transparency initiatives to public and social media discourse—are reshaping transparency practices in the Chinese court system. Methodologically, I combine natural language processing (including large language models) with causal inference to analyze large-scale textual and institutional datasets.

Prior to UCSD, I earned an M.S. in Electrical Engineering from Columbia University, with a focus on data analysis and machine learning. I worked as a Data Science Research Associate at Columbia Law School, where I collaborated with Benjamin Liebman at the Hong Yen Chang Center for Chinese Legal Studies.

My work has been published in journals such as Sociological Methods & Research, Comparative Political Studies, Asian Journal of Law and Society, and the Columbia Law Review. I am also a co-developer of ccmEstimator, an R package for comparative causal mediation analysis.

Outside of research, I enjoy climbing, playing the flute, and training my dog.

           

Research

Substantively, my research agenda centers on how emerging technologies and media platforms influence political communication and legal governance. Methodologically, I apply and develop advanced computational and statistical techniques to support data-rich social science and integrate tools from natural language processing, causal inference, and machine learning to study complex political and legal processes at scale.

Seeing the Shadow: Party Documents in Chinese Courts
Liebman B.L., Stern R.E., Gao W., Wu X, Roberts M.E.
Berkeley Journal of International Law, Forthcoming 2025.

This paper examines how Chinese courts handle internal Party documents, shedding light on the often invisible influence of political structures within legal adjudication.

Contesting and Controlling Abortion in China’s Courts
Bodurtha M., Liebman B.L., Li C., Wu X
Columbia Journal of Gender and Law, Forthcoming 2024.

This study explores how Chinese courts adjudicate abortion-related disputes, focusing on how law, gender, and social values interact in shaping judicial narratives and outcomes.

Rolling Back Transparency in China’s Courts
Liebman B.L., Stern R.E., Wu X, Roberts M.E.
Columbia Law Review, 2023.

This article documents the decline of judicial transparency in China since 2018 using court records and institutional analysis. It highlights shifts in public access to court decisions and their policy drivers.

Liability Beyond Law: Conceptions of Fairness in Chinese Tort Cases
Stern R.E., Liebman B.L., Gao W., Wu X
Asian Journal of Law and Society, 2023.

Using tort case data, we analyze how Chinese judges balance legal doctrine with moral and social reasoning, offering insights into localized understandings of justice and fairness in civil litigation.

Closing Open Government: Grassroots Policy Conversion of China’s Open Government Information Regulation and Its Aftermath
Kim J., Stern R.E., Liebman B.L., Wu X
Comparative Political Studies, 2022.

This paper examines how local actors reinterpret and adapt central transparency mandates, turning China's open government regulations into tools for grassroots legal mobilization.

On Constructing a Knowledge Base of Chinese Criminal Cases
Wu X, Liebman B.L., Stern R.E., Roberts M.E., Gupta A.
Proceedings of JURIX 2019, International Conference on Legal Knowledge and Information Systems.

We describe the design of a structured database of Chinese criminal cases, combining NLP methods and legal ontologies to support empirical analysis and data-driven legal scholarship.

How Recommendation Algorithms Affect Political Content Exposure: An Audit Study of YouTube
Wu X
Working paper

This project uses audit experiments to examine how YouTube's recommendation algorithm mediates exposure to political content. It evaluates content diversity and potential ideological biases in algorithmic curation.
